1. Project Overview

Context, objectives, and scope of the system.

1.1 Problem Statement

Gemini offers binary prediction contracts of the form "Will BTC break price K by time T?" These contracts pay $1 if the condition is met and $0 otherwise, making them equivalent to digital (binary/cash-or-nothing) options on BTC/USD. The market prices for these contracts may deviate from their theoretical fair value as implied by the current BTC spot price, volatility surface, and jump dynamics. This project aims to identify and exploit such mispricings systematically.

1.2 Core Thesis

The Bates (1996) stochastic volatility with jumps (SVJ) model provides a richer description of BTC price dynamics than Black-Scholes or even Heston alone. By calibrating a Bates model to observable market data (spot price, implied volatility surface, historical returns), we can derive theoretically fair prices for binary contracts and trade when the Gemini market price diverges beyond a statistically significant threshold.

1.3 Objectives

1.  Implement the Bates SVJ model calibrated to BTC/USD market data.

2.  Derive fair binary option prices from the calibrated model via Fourier inversion or Monte Carlo.

3.  Construct a real-time pipeline: spot data ingestion, model recalibration, contract pricing, and signal generation.

4.  Define a trading strategy with explicit entry/exit rules, position sizing, and risk controls.

5.  Backtest the strategy against historical Gemini contract data (or synthetic proxies).

6.  Deploy for paper trading, then live execution via Gemini API.

2. Theoretical Foundation

Mathematical specification of the Bates model and its application to binary option pricing.

2.1 The Bates (1996) SVJ Model

The Bates model extends the Heston stochastic volatility model by adding Merton-style log-normal jumps to the asset price process. Under the risk-neutral measure Q, the dynamics are:

dS(t) = (r - q - λm̅)S(t)dt + √v(t) S(t)dW₁(t) + S(t)dJ(t)

dv(t) = κ(θ - v(t))dt + σₕ√v(t) dW₂(t)

dW₁ · dW₂ = ρ dt

J(t): Compound Poisson, intensity λ, log-jump ~ N(μⱼ, σⱼ²)

m̅ = exp(μⱼ + σⱼ²/2) - 1 (compensator)

2.2 Parameter Definitions

  ---------------------- ----------------- -------------------------------------- ----------------------
  Parameter              Symbol            Description                            Typical BTC Range

  Spot price             S(t)              Current BTC/USD price                  Market observed

  Variance               v(t)              Instantaneous variance                 0.3–1.5 (annualized)

  Mean-reversion speed   κ                 Rate v(t) reverts to θ                 1.0–5.0

  Long-run variance      θ                 Equilibrium variance level             0.4–1.0

  Vol of vol             σₕ                Volatility of the variance process     0.5–2.0

  Correlation            ρ                 Spot–variance correlation (leverage)   -0.8 to -0.2

  Jump intensity         λ                 Expected jumps per year                1–20

  Jump mean              μⱼ                Mean of log-jump size                  -0.05 to 0.02

  Jump vol               σⱼ                Std dev of log-jump size               0.05–0.15
  ---------------------- ----------------- -------------------------------------- ----------------------

2.3 Characteristic Function

The Bates model admits a semi-analytic characteristic function φ(u) for log(S(T)), which has the Heston form augmented by a jump component:

φ(u) = φ_Heston(u) × exp(λ T [(1 + m̅)^{iu} exp(-μⱼu + σⱼ²u²/2) - 1 - iu m̅])

This characteristic function enables pricing via Fourier inversion (Gil-Pelaez, Carr-Madan, COS method) without Monte Carlo simulation, making real-time recalibration feasible.

2.4 Binary Option Pricing

A cash-or-nothing binary call paying $1 if S(T) > K is priced as:

C_bin(K,T) = e^{-rT} × Q(S(T) > K)

where Q(S(T) > K) is the risk-neutral probability recovered from the characteristic function via:

Q(S(T) > K) = 1/2 + (1/π) ∫₀^∞ Re[e^{-iu ln(K)} φ(u) / (iu)] du

For binary puts (paying $1 if S(T) < K): P_bin = e^{-rT} - C_bin.

4. Model Calibration

Procedures to estimate and continuously update all Bates model parameters.

4.1 Calibration Strategy

A two-stage calibration approach balances robustness with speed:

Stage 1 — Historical estimation (offline, daily): Estimate jump parameters (λ, μⱼ, σⱼ) from historical BTC returns using maximum likelihood on the jump-diffusion return density. Estimate ρ from rolling realized spot–variance correlation. These parameters are relatively stable and do not require intraday recalibration.

Stage 2 — Implied calibration (online, every 5–15 min): Calibrate (κ, θ, v₀, σₕ) by minimizing the weighted sum of squared errors between Bates model prices and observed Deribit option mid-prices across strikes and maturities. Use the Levenberg-Marquardt or differential evolution optimizer. Penalize parameters outside the feasible region via soft constraints.

4.2 Calibration Objective

min_Θ ∑ᵢ wᵢ (C_market(Kᵢ,Tᵢ) - C_Bates(Kᵢ,Tᵢ;Θ))² + α ‖Θ - Θ_prior‖²

where weights wᵢ emphasize at-the-money and near-term options (highest information content), and the regularization term anchors parameters to the previous calibration to prevent overfitting and ensure temporal smoothness.

4.3 Calibration Validation

-   Track calibration RMSE over time; flag regime changes when RMSE spikes above 2σ of its rolling mean.

-   Monitor Feller condition: 2κθ > σₕ² (ensures variance stays positive). Log warnings if violated.

-   Cross-validate by holding out 20% of option strikes and checking out-of-sample pricing error.

-   Compare implied vs realized jump frequency over rolling 30-day windows.

5. Pricing Engine

Methods for computing fair binary contract prices from calibrated parameters.

5.1 Primary Method: Fourier Inversion (COS Method)

The COS method (Fang & Oosterlee, 2008) is the recommended approach for production pricing due to its speed (O(N) complexity) and exponential convergence. Steps:

11. Compute the Bates characteristic function φ(u) at the current calibrated parameters.

12. Truncate the integration domain to [a, b] using cumulants of the log-price distribution.

13. Expand the density in a cosine series and compute the binary option price as a weighted sum of cosine coefficients.

14. N = 64–128 terms typically suffices for 6+ digits of accuracy.

5.2 Validation Method: Monte Carlo with Variance Reduction

Used to validate COS prices and for complex path-dependent contract structures if Gemini introduces them. Implementation details:

-   Euler-Maruyama or Milstein discretization of the joint (S, v) process with Poisson jump sampling.

-   Full truncation scheme for the variance process (set v = max(v, 0) in the diffusion coefficient).

-   Antithetic variates and control variates (use vanilla European option with known Bates price as control).

-   10⁵–10⁶ paths for benchmark accuracy; 10⁴ paths for quick sanity checks.

6. Trading Strategy

Signal generation, execution logic, and position management.

6.1 Mispricing Signal

For each active Gemini binary contract with strike K and expiry T, compute:

mispricing(K,T) = P_market(K,T) - P_fair(K,T;Θ)

where P_market is the Gemini contract mid-price and P_fair is the Bates model fair value. Normalize by model-implied standard error:

z(K,T) = mispricing(K,T) / σ_model(K,T)

where σ_model is estimated from the sensitivity of the contract price to calibration parameter uncertainty (propagated via the Jacobian of the pricing function).

6.2 Entry Rules

-   Enter long (buy contract) when z < -z_threshold (contract is underpriced).

-   Enter short (sell contract) when z > +z_threshold (contract is overpriced).

-   Default z_threshold = 2.0; optimize via backtest. Higher threshold = fewer trades, higher expected edge per trade.

-   Require minimum model confidence: calibration RMSE below threshold and Feller condition satisfied.

-   Require minimum time-to-expiry (e.g., > 2 hours) to avoid gamma risk near expiration.

6.3 Exit Rules

-   Target exit when |z| < 0.5 (mispricing has mean-reverted).

-   Stop-loss: exit if unrealized loss exceeds 2x the expected edge at entry.

-   Time stop: flatten position if T_remaining < 30 minutes (binary gamma becomes unmanageable).

-   Model stop: flatten all positions if calibration RMSE exceeds emergency threshold.

6.4 Position Sizing

Use a Kelly-criterion-derived fractional sizing approach:

f* = (p̂ × b - (1 - p̂)) / b

where p̂ is the model-implied probability of profit and b is the contract payoff ratio. Apply a fractional Kelly multiplier (0.25–0.5) to account for model uncertainty. Maximum position size per contract: 5% of portfolio NAV. Maximum aggregate exposure: 25% of NAV.

7. Risk Management

Comprehensive risk controls and monitoring framework.

7.1 Model Risk

-   Run parallel pricing under alternative models (Black-Scholes, Heston without jumps, SABR) and only trade when all models agree on the direction of mispricing.

-   Monitor jump parameter stability: if λ or σⱼ shift by > 50% in a single recalibration, halt trading and investigate.

-   Maintain a model confidence score based on out-of-sample pricing error, Feller condition margin, and parameter stability.

7.2 Execution Risk

-   Account for bid-ask spread on Gemini contracts; only trade when mispricing exceeds spread + transaction costs.

-   Implement latency monitoring: if data feed lag exceeds 5 seconds, suspend signal generation.

-   Rate-limit order submissions per Gemini API limits.

12. Extension Roadmap

Future enhancements beyond the initial deployment.

-   Regime-switching Bates model: Incorporate Markov-switching for κ, θ to handle structural breaks in BTC volatility dynamics.

-   Machine learning residual model: Train a gradient-boosted model on calibration residuals to capture systematic model bias as a function of market microstructure features.

-   Multi-asset expansion: Extend to ETH/USD or other crypto prediction contracts if Gemini offers them.

-   Implied risk-neutral density extraction: Visualize the full model-implied density vs market-implied density to identify distributional mispricings beyond point prices.

-   Bayesian calibration: Replace point estimation with MCMC posterior over Θ to obtain principled uncertainty quantification on fair prices.

-   Market making mode: Instead of directional bets on mispricing, quote two-sided markets on contracts, earning the bid-ask spread while hedging with BTC spot or perpetuals.

Appendix B: LLM Implementation Notes

Guidance for LLMs tasked with implementing components of this system.

Pricing module: Implement the Bates characteristic function exactly as specified in Section 2.3. Use the Fang-Oosterlee COS method for the binary option price integral. The integration truncation bounds should be computed from the first four cumulants of the Bates log-price distribution. Validate against Monte Carlo with 10⁶ paths.

Calibration module: Use scipy.optimize.differential_evolution for the global search, then polish with scipy.optimize.minimize (L-BFGS-B). The objective function is defined in Section 4.2. Enforce parameter bounds: κ ∈ [0.1, 10], θ ∈ [0.01, 3], σₕ ∈ [0.1, 5], ρ ∈ [-0.99, 0.99], v₀ ∈ [0.01, 3], λ ∈ [0, 50], μⱼ ∈ [-0.2, 0.2], σⱼ ∈ [0.01, 0.5].

Signal generation: The z-score formula in Section 6.1 requires propagating parameter uncertainty through the pricing function. Compute σ_model via delta-method: σ_model² = Jᵀ Σ J, where J is the Jacobian of the pricing function w.r.t. Θ (computed by finite differences) and Σ is the covariance matrix of the calibrated parameters (from the inverse Hessian at the optimum).

Backtesting: Do not use future information. Calibrate only on data available at each historical timestamp. Account for execution lag (assume 1–2 second fill delay). Include transaction costs as a fixed percentage of contract notional. Use walk-forward optimization: train on 60 days, test on 20 days, roll forward.
